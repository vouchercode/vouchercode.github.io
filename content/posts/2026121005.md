## Immediate Security Audit: Your 3-Step Checklist (Must Complete Tonight)

Do not wait. Follow this actionable checklist immediately.

### Step 1: Review and Update Your Withdrawal Address Whitelist
Navigate to **Security > Withdrawal Address Management** in your Binance account.
*   **Action**: Scrutinize every whitelisted address. Remove any addresses for wallets you no longer use or do not recognize.
*   **Critical Tip**: For added security, consider disabling the whitelist temporarily for 24 hours after the audit, requiring manual approval for *every* withdrawal. Re-enable it only after adding freshly verified addresses.
*   **The $500 Risk**: An old, compromised address sitting in your whitelist is a direct pipeline for draining your funds. Cleaning this list closes that door.

### Step 2: Upgrade Your Two-Factor Authentication (2FA) NOW
SMS-based 2FA is obsolete and highly vulnerable to SIM-swap attacks. Your 2FA method is your last line of defense.
*   **Action**: Go to **Security > 2FA Verification**. Disable SMS 2FA for withdrawals and enable an authenticator app (Google Authenticator or Authy) or a hardware security key (YubiKey).
*   **Why This Can't Wait**: The process to change 2FA has a mandatory waiting period (often 24-48 hours). Initiating this upgrade *tonight* means it will be fully active before the peak of the post-announcement attack wave, locking down your account during the most dangerous period.

### Step 3: Verify Account Activity and API Keys
*   **Check Login History**: Go to **Security > Account Activity**. Look for any unrecognized devices or locations. Log them out immediately.
*   **Audit API Keys**: Navigate to **API Management**. Delete any API keys you are not actively using, especially those with "Enable Withdrawals" permissions. For necessary keys, restrict permissions to "Read Only" or "Spot & Margin Trading" only, and set a strict IP access whitelist.

## The Hidden Threat: How Attackers Bypass "Secure" Accounts

You might think your account is secure, but modern attacks are sophisticated. Here’s how they circumvent standard protections:

1.  **Cookie Hijacking & Session Attacks**: Malware can steal your active browser session, allowing attackers to act as "you" without needing your password or 2FA initially. Regularly clearing cookies and using secure browsers is essential.
2.  **Fake Support Impersonation**: A call or message from "Binance Support" claiming your account is compromised and they need you to disable 2FA "temporarily" to secure it. **Binance support will NEVER ask you to disable 2FA.**
3.  **Withdrawal Address Poisoning**: Malware on your device can silently replace the withdrawal address you paste from your clipboard with the attacker's address. Always double-check, character-by-character, the first and last five digits of any address before confirming a withdrawal.

## FAQ: Urgent Security Questions Answered

**Q: I just updated my security last month. Do I really need to do this again?**
A: **Yes.** Security is not a one-time setup; it's an ongoing process. The Fed announcement creates a unique, high-threat environment that demands a proactive review. An address whitelisted last month could be compromised today.

**Q: What if I'm locked out during the 2FA change waiting period?**
A: The waiting period is a security feature. Plan your upgrade during a time you won't need to make urgent withdrawals. The short-term inconvenience is vastly preferable to the permanent loss of funds. Ensure your registered email and phone number are up-to-date before starting the process.

**Q: Is a $500 loss estimate realistic, or is it fear-mongering?**
A: It is a conservative estimate. Attackers often test with small withdrawals first. If successful, they will attempt to drain the entire account. The $500 represents the initial, often undetected, "probing" loss that precedes a total account compromise.

**Q: I use a hardware wallet. Am I safe?**
A: Your hardware wallet is secure, but the *withdrawal process from Binance to it* is not. If your Binance account is compromised, the attacker withdraws *your* funds to *their* wallet. The security of your destination wallet doesn't matter if the sending account is breached.
