## How This "Standard" Clause Creates Unseen Risks

### Risk 1: Irreversible Biometric Exposure in a Data Breach
Passwords and 2FA codes can be reset. Biometric data, once leaked, is compromised forever. If a hacker accesses a database containing the "derivative" facial recognition templates generated from user verification photos, that data could be weaponized for identity theft across other platforms that use similar biometric checks, not just in crypto.

### Risk 2: Loss of Control Over Personal Data Trajectory
The clause potentially severs the user's control chain. Your photo and its biometric derivatives could be analyzed by AI models, shared with subcontractors in different jurisdictions with varying data protection laws, all under the umbrella of "fraud prevention." This creates a complex, opaque trail that is impossible for an individual user to audit or retract.

### Risk 3: Ambiguity in "Security Purpose" Definitions
What constitutes a "security purpose" sufficient to trigger data sharing with third parties? The broad wording could allow for significant discretion. While intended for legitimate investigations, the lack of precise definition creates a gray area that potentially expands the use case of your sensitive data beyond what a reasonable user might expect when simply trying to verify their account.

### Risk 4: The Illusion of "Completed" Security
This scenario creates a dangerous complacency. A user who has painstakingly completed KYC, set up 2FA, and uses a hardware wallet rightly feels secure. They are unaware that the very process that unlocked their account's full functionality may have introduced a latent, systemic vulnerability tied to their immutable biometric identity, undermining their other meticulous security efforts.
