## Why is Monitoring OI, Liquidation Levels, and IV Non-Negotiable?

Understanding these three pillars is akin to having a real-time diagnostic of the market's health, leverage saturation, and potential stress points. They are not just numbers but narratives of crowd psychology and mechanical triggers.

*   **Open Interest (OI): The Commitment Gauge.** OI represents the total number of outstanding derivative contracts (like futures or options) that have not been settled. Rising OI alongside a price trend suggests new money is fueling the move, indicating strong conviction. Conversely, falling OI during a trend may signal its exhaustion. Monitoring OI by expiry and strike price reveals where institutional positions are concentrated.
*   **Liquidation Levels: The Market's Fault Lines.** These are the price levels where leveraged positions are automatically closed by exchanges due to insufficient margin. High concentrations of liquidation orders act as magnets for price action, often leading to cascading liquidations and exacerbated volatility. Identifying these clusters allows traders to anticipate potential "stop-hunts" or liquidity squeezes.
*   **Implied Volatility (IV): The Market's Fear & Greed Index.** IV, derived from options prices, reflects the market's expectation of future price swings. High IV suggests uncertainty and potential for large moves (often around major events), while low IV indicates complacency. For options traders, IV is a direct input into pricing models and a key determinant of strategy selection (e.g., selling premium in high IV environments).
